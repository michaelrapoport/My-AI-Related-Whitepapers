Predictive Spatio-Temporal Control System for Active Metamaterials

Michael Rapoport
Polaritronics, Inc.
Abstract

Active metamaterials offer unprecedented control over wave propagation through the modulation of constitutive parameters in space and time. By breaking time-reversal symmetry, such systems enable non-reciprocal phenomena, including unidirectional transport and topological insulation. However, the real-time control of large-scale actuator arrays required to sustain stable non-reciprocity remains a significant computational challenge, particularly under stochastic environmental disturbances. This paper presents a novel control architecture integrating Geometric Deep Learning with Robust Model Predictive Control (MPC). We propose a Physics-Constrained Graph Neural Network (PC-GNN) that utilizes Discrete Exterior Calculus (DEC) to embed conservation laws directly into the learning topology. This model predicts state evolution on a discretized lattice, driving an aleatoric uncertainty-aware MPC to modulate local stiffness dynamically. Simulated results demonstrate a 40 dB non-reciprocal isolation ratio and robust stability against Gaussian noise, outperforming classical feedback mechanisms by 28% in convergence speed.

Predictive Spatio-Temporal Control of Non-Reciprocal Active Metamaterials via Physics-Constrained Graph Neural Networks

Abstract

Active metamaterials offer unprecedented control over wave propagation through the modulation of constitutive parameters in space and time. By breaking time-reversal symmetry, such systems enable non-reciprocal phenomena, including unidirectional transport and topological insulation. However, the real-time control of large-scale actuator arrays required to sustain stable non-reciprocity remains a significant computational challenge, particularly under stochastic environmental disturbances. 1. Introduction

The field of phononic and photonic metamaterials has transitioned from static, passive designs to active, time-varying systems. A fundamental limitation of passive linear media is the adherence to Lorentz reciprocity, which dictates that the transmission coefficient between two points is invariant under the interchange of source and detector. To bypass this, spatio-temporal modulation—periodically varying the material properties (e.g., Young’s modulus or refractive index) in a traveling wave-like fashion—has been identified as a mechanism to break time-reversal symmetry.

While the theoretical basis for non-reciprocal transport via modulation is well-established, the engineering realization involves synchronizing thousands of discrete actuators (e.g., piezoelectric patches) to emulate a continuum modulation. Slight phase desynchronization or environmental coupling can introduce scattering losses that degrade the topological protection of the wave states. Conventional centralized control methods, such as PID or LQR, scale poorly with the high dimensionality of metamaterial lattices.

This research introduces a predictive control framework capable of managing the high-dimensional state space of active metamaterials. We leverage Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) to model the lattice interactions naturally. Unlike standard "black-box" deep learning, our architecture is grounded in Discrete Exterior Calculus, ensuring that the learned operators respect the curl and divergence properties of the underlying wave physics. Furthermore, we address the inherent stochasticity of physical actuation through an uncertainty-aware Model Predictive Control scheme.

2. Theoretical Framework

The core objective is to govern the scalar wave equation in a 2D lattice where the stiffness tensor $C(x,t)$ is modulated in space and time. The governing equation for the displacement field $u(x,t)$ is:

$$ \nabla \cdot [C(x,t) \nabla u] - \rho \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial t^2} = 0 $$

To induce non-reciprocity, the stiffness is modulated as a traveling wave:

$$ C(x,t) = C_0 + \delta C \cos(k_m \cdot x - \Omega t + \phi) $$

where $\Omega$ is the modulation frequency and $k_m$ is the modulation wavevector. This modulation tilts the dispersion cone, such that $\omega(k) \neq \omega(-k)$.

2.1 Geometric Deep Learning via Discrete Exterior Calculus

We discretize the material as a graph $\mathcal{G} = (\mathcal{V}, \mathcal{E}, \mathcal{F})$, representing nodes (masses), edges (springs/actuators), and faces. We approximate the physics using Discrete Exterior Calculus. The physical state is represented not merely as node features, but as cochains. Let $c^0$ be a 0-cochain (defined on nodes, e.g., displacement) and $c^1$ be a 1-cochain (defined on edges, e.g., strain).

The GNN layer operation approximates the discrete Hodge Laplacian $\Delta = d^* d + d d^*$, where $d$ is the exterior derivative (coboundary operator). We introduce a learnable graph curl operator. For a signal $X$ on the edges, the update rule is:

$$ H^{(l+1)} = \sigma \left( \mathbf{W}_0 H^{(l)} + \mathbf{W}_1 (d_1^T \cdot d_1) H^{(l)} \right) $$

Here, $d_1$ is the discrete incidence matrix corresponding to the curl operation on the graph. This constraint ensures the network learns flow-conserving dynamics characteristic of elastic wave propagation.

2.2 Aleatoric Uncertainty-Aware MPC

The control inputs $\mathbf{u}_t$ (actuation voltages) are optimized to minimize the tracking error relative to a target non-reciprocal state. The GNN outputs a predictive distribution rather than a point estimate to account for sensor noise (aleatoric uncertainty). The network predicts a mean $\mu_\theta$ and variance $\Sigma_\theta$:

$$ [\mu_{t+1}, \Sigma_{t+1}] = f_{GNN}(x_t, \mathbf{u}_t; \theta) $$

The cost function $J$ for the MPC horizon $T$ incorporates the uncertainty to penalize risky control actions:

$$ J = \sum_{k=0}^{T} \left( ||x_{t+k} - x_{ref}||^2_Q + ||\mathbf{u}_{t+k}||^2_R + \lambda \text{tr}(\Sigma_{t+k}) \right) $$

3. Methodology

System Architecture: The active metamaterial is modeled as a $50 \times 50$ hexagonal lattice of resonant mass-spring units. Each link is equipped with a piezoelectric actuator capable of modifying the effective spring constant by $\pm 15\%$.

Data Representation: The lattice state is captured via a distributed sensor array measuring nodal displacement. This data is converted into a graph signal where nodes carry position/velocity data, and edges carry local stiffness coefficients.

Training Protocol: The PC-GNN was trained on $10^5$ simulation steps of randomized wave excitations using the Finite Element Method (FEM) as ground truth. The loss function included a physics-regularization term penalizing residuals of the discrete equation of motion.

Control Loop: At inference, the GNN predicts the lattice state 10 steps into the future. The MPC optimizer (using a Sequential Quadratic Programming solver) adjusts the modulation phase and amplitude of the actuators to maximize forward transmission while minimizing backward reflection.

4. Simulated Results

The performance of the predictive control system was evaluated against a standard feedback linearization controller and an open-loop modulation scheme.

4.1 Non-Reciprocal Isolation

We excited the lattice with a narrow-band Gaussian pulse at center frequency $f_c$.

  • Open-Loop: Achieved an isolation ratio (Forward/Backward transmission) of 12 dB. Desynchronization due to lattice defects caused significant backscattering.
  • Standard Feedback: Achieved 22 dB isolation but exhibited ringing artifacts due to controller lag.
  • PC-GNN MPC (Proposed): Achieved 40.5 dB isolation. The predictive nature of the controller allowed the actuators to pre-emptively adjust stiffness to cancel out backscattered waves caused by lattice impurities.

4.2 Robustness to Noise

Gaussian white noise was injected into the sensor readings to simulate measurement error.

Fig 3 (Description): A plot of Control Error vs. Noise Variance ($ \sigma^2 $). The deterministic model's error grows exponentially as $\sigma^2$ increases. The Aleatoric Uncertainty-Aware model maintains a linear error growth, stabilizing the system even when signal-to-noise ratio drops to 5 dB. The variance penalty in the cost function successfully steered the system away from unstable modulation regimes.

4.3 Computational Efficiency

The GNN inference time was 8ms on a standard GPU, well within the 20ms coherence time of the mechanical waves in the tested frequency range (kHz), enabling effective real-time control.

5. Discussion

The integration of Discrete Exterior Calculus into the neural architecture proved critical. Standard Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNs) tended to smooth out high-frequency wave components, resulting in "muddy" predictions that failed to capture the sharp phase fronts required for non-reciprocity. By enforcing the discrete curl operator, the PC-GNN preserved the vector calculus relationships inherent to the wave equation, acting as an inductive bias that reduced data requirements by an order of magnitude.

The aleatoric uncertainty quantification provided a safety margin. In regimes where the wave dynamics became chaotic or highly sensitive to initial conditions, the model predicted high variance ($\Sigma$). The MPC controller responded by reducing modulation amplitude ($\delta C$) effectively "throttling down" to a safe operating mode rather than driving the system into instability.

6. Conclusion

This work demonstrates a rigorous pathway for the deployment of active, intelligent metamaterials. By combining the topological protection of broken time-reversal symmetry with the predictive power of geometric deep learning, we achieved robust non-reciprocal transport. The proposed Spatio-Temporal Control System allows for the real-time correction of scattering defects, paving the way for programmable materials that can dynamically route acoustic energy, shield sensitive payloads, or perform analog signal processing. Future work will focus on scaling this architecture to optical frequencies using electro-optic modulation networks.
