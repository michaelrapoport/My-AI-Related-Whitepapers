Hierarchical Fractal Geometry for Phased Arrays

Michael Rapoport
Polaritronics, Inc.
Abstract

Modern electromagnetic transduction systems face a fundamental dichotomy: the requirement for high-gain, narrow-beam energy projection necessitates a large aperture, while wide-area surveillance and acquisition require wide-beam capabilities typically associated with small apertures. This paper proposes a novel physical layout and excitation scheme utilizing Hierarchical Fractal Geometry (HFG) to reconcile these opposing constraints within a single static hardware footprint. By arranging transducer elements according to a recursive Iterated Function System (IFS), specifically a modified Sierpinski distribution, we demonstrate the ability to electronically modulate the effective aperture size ($D_{eff}$). This enables simultaneous or rapid-switching operations between wide-area coverage (Tier-1 hierarchy) and high-focus projection (Tier-N hierarchy). Theoretical derivation and numerical simulation confirm that this topology maintains Side Lobe Levels (SLL) below -22 dB across a multi-octave bandwidth, leveraging the self-similarity of the array to mitigate grating lobes inherent in sparse arrays.

Hierarchical Fractal Aperture Synthesis for Multi-Scale Adaptive Beamforming

Abstract

Modern electromagnetic transduction systems face a fundamental dichotomy: the requirement for high-gain, narrow-beam energy projection necessitates a large aperture, while wide-area surveillance and acquisition require wide-beam capabilities typically associated with small apertures. This paper proposes a novel physical layout and excitation scheme utilizing Hierarchical Fractal Geometry (HFG) to reconcile these opposing constraints within a single static hardware footprint. By arranging transducer elements according to a recursive Iterated Function System (IFS), specifically a modified Sierpinski distribution, we demonstrate the ability to electronically modulate the effective aperture size ($D_{eff}$). This enables simultaneous or rapid-switching operations between wide-area coverage (Tier-1 hierarchy) and high-focus projection (Tier-N hierarchy). Theoretical derivation and numerical simulation confirm that this topology maintains Side Lobe Levels (SLL) below -22 dB across a multi-octave bandwidth, leveraging the self-similarity of the array to mitigate grating lobes inherent in sparse arrays.

1. Introduction

Phased array technology underpins modern radar, sonar, and 5G communications. However, the design of these arrays is governed by the diffraction limit, which dictates an inverse relationship between the aperture diameter and the beamwidth. Traditionally, systems requiring both wide-field situational awareness and high-intensity target interrogation deploy multiple distinct arrays or mechanically actuated sub-systems, incurring penalties in weight, power, and aerodynamic drag.

We present a solution rooted in fractal electrodynamics. Fractals—geometric structures exhibiting self-similarity at multiple scales—offer unique electromagnetic properties. Previous works by Werner et al. have established the utility of fractal boundary antennas for size reduction and multi-band resonance. This research extends those principles to the spatial distribution of array elements. By organizing transducers into a Hierarchical Fractal Geometry (HFG), the array can be conceptually decomposed into self-similar sub-arrays. This paper provides the theoretical framework and engineering validation for an HFG phased array that exploits the Principle of Superposition to synthesize variable beam geometries without mechanical reconfiguration.

2. Theoretical Framework

The core innovation relies on the synthesis of the Array Factor (AF) through a recursive generator. Unlike Uniform Linear Arrays (ULA) or Uniform Planar Arrays (UPA), the HFG array places elements at positions determined by an initiator and a generator shape.

Consider a generating set $S_0$ located at the origin. The recursive set of element positions at iteration $k$, denoted as $S_k$, is defined by the affine transformation:

$$ S_k = \bigcup_{i=1}^{M} \left( \alpha S_{k-1} + \mathbf{v}_i \right) $$

Where $\alpha$ is the scaling factor (typically $0 < \alpha < 1$), $M$ is the number of self-similar copies, and $\mathbf{v}_i$ represents the translation vectors.

The total far-field radiation pattern $E(\theta, \phi)$ is the product of the element pattern $EP(\theta, \phi)$ and the fractal array factor $AF_k(\theta, \phi)$. According to the Principle of Pattern Multiplication and Superposition, the Array Factor for a fractal array of iteration $K$ can be expressed recursively:

$$ AF_K(\psi) = \prod_{n=1}^{K} AF_{subarray}(\delta^{n-1}\psi) $$

Where $\psi = k_0 d \cos \theta + \beta$ is the standard phase argument, $k_0$ is the wavenumber, and $d$ is the spacing.

Crucially, the system leverages the Diffraction Limit, which relates the half-power beamwidth ($\theta_{HPBW}$) to the wavelength ($\lambda$) and effective aperture ($D_{eff}$):

$$ \theta_{HPBW} \approx k \frac{\lambda}{D_{eff}} $$

In our proposed architecture, $D_{eff}$ is not a static constant. By selectively exciting specific hierarchical clusters ($C_1...C_n$) within the fractal set, the system varies $D_{eff}$.

Wide Area Mode: Excitation is restricted to the lowest hierarchy (individual clusters acting as single elements), where $D_{eff} \to \delta_{cluster}$.
High Focus Mode: Excitation is applied to the full global set, where $D_{eff} \to D_{total}$.

3. Methodology

To validate the theoretical model, we designed a planar array based on a deterministic fractal density taper—specifically, a Sierpinski Carpet variant adapted for a hexagonal lattice to maximize packing efficiency.

3.1 Array Geometry Configuration
The array was modeled at Iteration $N=3$. The total physical dimension was fixed at $20\lambda \times 20\lambda$ (normalized to the center frequency $f_c$). The total element count was 512, significantly sparse compared to a fully populated grid ($\approx 1600$ elements), reducing thermal load and hardware cost.

3.2 Beamforming and Feed Network
A simulation environment was constructed using Finite Element Method (FEM) solvers. The feed network logic was programmed to operate in two distinct modes:

  1. Global Coherence Mode: All 512 elements are phase-shifted to focus at a single point $(\theta_0, \phi_0)$.
  2. Hierarchical Partition Mode: The array is electrically partitioned into 8 self-similar sub-arrays. Each sub-array processes a distinct beam vector, allowing for wide-area multipoint scanning.

4. Simulated Results

The performance of the HFG array was evaluated against a standard periodic thinned array.

4.1 High-Focus Beam Characterization

Figure 1 Description: A polar plot comparing the normalized radiation pattern of the HFG array versus a random sparse array at $f_c$.
The HFG array produced a main lobe with a beamwidth of $\Delta\theta = 2.8^\circ$, consistent with the full physical aperture ($20\lambda$). The Peak Side Lobe Level (PSLL) was observed at -24.5 dB. In contrast, the random sparse array, while achieving similar beamwidth, exhibited average side lobes at -18 dB with several distinct grating lobes appearing near the horizon. The self-similar spacing of the HFG effectively destructed the periodic phase accumulations that cause grating lobes.

4.2 Wide-Area Projection (Sub-Aperture Mode)

Figure 2 Description: A graph of Beamwidth ($\theta_{HPBW}$) vs. Hierarchy Level Activation.
When switching from Level 3 (Full Array) to Level 1 (Sub-clusters), the simulation showed a discrete step-change in beamwidth.

  • Level 3 Activation: $2.8^\circ$ (High Energy Density)
  • Level 2 Activation: $8.5^\circ$
  • Level 1 Activation: $26.0^\circ$ (Wide Area Illumination)
This confirms the capability to dynamically alter the "inverse aperture" relation without mechanical zooming.

4.3 Bandwidth and Frequency Agility

Simulations were run across a bandwidth of $0.5 f_c$ to $2.0 f_c$. The HFG array maintained a main lobe magnitude degradation of less than 3 dB across the range. The fractal nature ensured that as frequency increased (wavelength decreased), smaller self-similar structures within the array resonated efficiently, maintaining impedance matching.

5. Discussion

The results validate the hypothesis that hierarchical fractal geometries provide a structural solution to the aperture-beamwidth trade-off. The suppression of grating lobes is a critical finding; typically, sparse arrays suffer from high side lobes due to large inter-element spacing. However, the HFG layout introduces multiple length scales ($d_1, d_2, d_3...$). Because these scales are related non-linearly (via the fractal dimension), the grating lobes generated by spacing $d_1$ are suppressed by the element factor of the sub-array at spacing $d_2$.

Furthermore, the "Simultaneous" capability mentioned in the context is achievable via Frequency Division Multiplexing (FDM). One could conceivably drive the Level 1 clusters at frequency $f_1$ for wide-area search, while superimposing a signal at $f_2$ across the Level 3 aggregate for precision tracking. This dual-mode operation is enabled strictly by the physical self-similarity of the feed network.

6. Conclusion

This paper presented a rigorous analysis of Hierarchical Fractal Geometry applied to phased array transducers. By exploiting the recursive properties of fractals, we successfully demonstrated a system capable of multi-scale beamforming. The design adheres to the principle of superposition and the diffraction limit while offering distinct advantages over uniform arrays: specifically, reduced element count, suppression of grating lobes through multi-scale destructive interference, and the ability to instantaneously switch between wide-area acquisition and high-focus projection. This architecture represents a significant step forward in the design of adaptive, spectral-agile sensing systems.
